01
What is phishing?
// The anatomy of a social engineering attack
In progress

Phishing is manipulation, not malware.

Phishing attacks don't break through your defenses — they walk right past them by targeting human judgment. The attacker's goal is to get you to act before your critical thinking engages: click a link, enter credentials, transfer funds, or open an attachment.

Unlike technical exploits, phishing works regardless of how up-to-date your systems are. Your behavior is the control being bypassed.

For Executives & Partners: Senior leaders are the most valuable target. Attackers research your title, authority, and relationships to craft highly personalized "spear phishing" emails that appear to come from board members, auditors, or regulators.

For Finance & Accounting: Your role makes you a primary target for Business Email Compromise — attackers impersonating executives to authorize wire transfers, redirect payroll, or share financial data.

For HR & People Ops: HR is targeted for W-2 forms, direct deposit changes, and employee PII. Attackers often impersonate executives requesting employee data urgently before an "audit."

For Legal & Compliance: You handle confidential agreements, M&A data, and privileged communications — all high-value targets. Attackers may impersonate opposing counsel, regulators, or court systems.

For IT & Security: You hold privileged access. Attackers target IT staff specifically to gain admin credentials — a "helpdesk" call asking you to reset a VPN token for an urgent issue is a classic vector.

For Developers: Developer accounts have access to source code, CI/CD pipelines, and cloud infrastructure. A compromised developer account can be used to inject malicious code or access production secrets.

📧 Email phishing

Mass or targeted emails impersonating trusted brands, colleagues, or authorities.

🎯 Spear phishing

Highly targeted with personal details — name, role, org, recent events — to appear legitimate.

📱 Smishing / Vishing

Same tactics via SMS or phone. "This is IT support — your account has been flagged."

👔 Business Email Compromise

Impersonating a CEO or CFO to authorize wire transfers or share confidential data.

02
How to spot a phishing attack
// Six signals every target misses
Locked

Every attack leaves traces. Urgency makes you skip the checks that catch them.

Phishing emails are designed to create pressure that overrides careful thinking. The signals are consistent — once you know them, they're hard to miss.

1. Sender domain mismatch

Display name says "Microsoft" — actual domain is microsoft-secure.net. Always check the real sending address.

2. Artificial urgency

"Act within 24 hours or lose access." Urgency is the primary manipulation lever. Legitimate services rarely threaten immediate lockout.

3. URL mismatch

Hover before clicking. The real domain is the part just before the first slash after https://. Subdomains before it can be anything.

4. Unexpected request

Did you initiate this? If you didn't request a password reset, why did one arrive?

5. Generic or suspicious greeting

"Dear Customer" is suspicious. Even your name doesn't confirm legitimacy — attackers harvest names from data breaches.

6. No MFA prompt on "login"

Real logins to modern platforms prompt for multi-factor authentication. A page that only asks for a password is a red flag.

Finance red flags: Payment redirect requests, invoice "discrepancies" requiring urgent action, and CEO/CFO impersonation are the most common vectors targeting your department.

IT/Security red flags: Attackers targeting IT often use "helpdesk escalation" themes — urgent tickets, system alerts, or vendor security notifications requesting credential verification.

Legal red flags: Watch for fake court notifications, "encrypted document" sharing from opposing counsel, and bar association compliance notices requiring login.

03
Data classification & protection
// Not all data carries the same risk
Locked

Knowing what you're protecting changes how you protect it.

Organizations classify data to match the level of protection to the level of risk.

  • Confidential / Restricted: Trade secrets, M&A data, source code, legal privileged communications, patient records, credentials. Requires encryption at rest and in transit, strict access controls, audit logging.
  • Personal Information (PI): Any information about an identified or identifiable individual — name, email, address, phone. Subject to privacy regulations.
  • Sensitive Personal Information (SPI): Subset of PI where misuse causes greater harm — health records, financial account numbers, SSNs. Treated as Confidential.
  • Regulated Information: Payment card data (PCI-DSS), health records (HIPAA), financial records (SOX). Additional controls apply.
  • Internal: Business information not for public disclosure but not highly sensitive — memos, process docs.
  • Public: Intentionally released — press releases, published website content.

For HR: Employee records contain both PI and SPI. W-2 requests, direct deposit changes, and employment verification require strict verification procedures.

For Finance: Payment card data, bank account information, and financial statements all carry regulatory requirements. Transmitting this data outside encrypted channels is a compliance violation.

For Legal: Privileged communications represent some of the most sensitive Confidential data. Accidental disclosure — wrong recipient, unencrypted attachments — can waive privilege.

04
Access control fundamentals
// Who can access what — and why it matters
Locked

Access should be earned, scoped, and regularly reviewed.

Most breaches involve either compromised credentials or access that should have been revoked.

  • Need-to-know / Least Privilege: Access is granted only for what a role requires. An accountant doesn't need source code access.
  • Unique identities: Every person must have their own account. Shared accounts make accountability impossible.
  • Timely deprovisioning: Access must be removed within 24 hours of someone leaving or changing roles.
  • Periodic review: Access rights must be reviewed at least annually.
  • Privileged access: Admin access requires stricter controls — separate accounts, enhanced logging, more frequent review.

For IT & Security: Common failures include: access requests approved without documented business need, accounts not disabled within SLA after termination, and shared administrative passwords. Each is an audit finding and a breach risk.

For Executives: Your accounts are high-value targets. If you use a shared assistant account, an attacker who compromises the assistant gains your access without triggering your MFA.

Passwords, authentication & MFA
// The first line of identity defense
Locked

A password alone is not enough.

Passwords are regularly compromised through phishing, credential stuffing, and brute-force. MFA ensures a stolen password alone cannot provide access.

  • Minimum 15 characters — length is the primary driver of strength.
  • Mix of at least 2 character types.
  • Changed at minimum every 90 days or immediately upon any suspicion of compromise.
  • Unique per system — reuse means one breach compromises all others.
  • Never stored in clear text — use a password manager.
  • Never shared with colleagues, assistants, or family members.

MFA fatigue attacks: If attackers have your password, they can repeatedly trigger MFA prompts hoping you'll approve one to make them stop. Never approve an MFA prompt you didn't initiate. Report unexpected prompts to IT immediately.

Acceptable use & device security
// Your device is an attack surface
Locked

The device in your hands is a gateway to your entire organization.

  • No unauthorized software.
  • No circumventing security controls — antivirus, EDR, VPN, screen lock, encryption.
  • No personal email for business — no DLP, no encryption enforcement, no audit logging.
  • Accept patches promptly — deferral leaves known exploitable flaws in place.
  • Screen lock within 15 minutes.
  • No auto-forwarding to personal email — a common data exfiltration vector.
  • Encrypt portable storage — lost unencrypted media is a reportable breach event.

Incident response — your role
// What to do when something goes wrong
Locked

Your first action determines whether a bad event becomes a catastrophic one.

  • Report immediately — every hour of delay is an hour the attacker has to expand access or exfiltrate data.
  • Do not investigate independently — unless directed by the security team. Independent action can destroy forensic evidence.
  • Do not patch or modify the affected asset — preserve the state for forensic analysis.
  • Do not discuss outside secure channels — attackers who've breached email may be monitoring.
  • All suspected incidents are reportable — false alarms cost nothing. Missed incidents are expensive.

Regulatory timelines: Some incidents trigger mandatory external reporting — GDPR's 72-hour window, state breach notification laws. The clock starts when the organization becomes aware, not when the security team is notified.

Your personal security playbook
// Habits that make you hard to phish
Locked

Security is a habit, not an event.

🛑 Stop before acting

When anything creates urgency around access, money, or data — treat that urgency as the red flag, not a reason to hurry.

🔍 Verify out-of-band

For anything involving funds, credentials, or sensitive data — call a number you already have. Not from the message.

📢 Report everything suspicious

Forward phishing emails to your security team. Report unexpected MFA prompts, strange account behavior. False alarms cost nothing.

🔒 Unique passwords + MFA everywhere

A password manager solves reuse. MFA means one stolen password doesn't compromise an account.

🔐 Lock before leaving

Lock your screen every time you step away — desk, coffee shop, conference room. Always.

📱 Think before you click

Hover over links. Check the real sender domain. Ask: did I initiate this? If anything is off — navigate directly.
